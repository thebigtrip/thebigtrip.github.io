Monday, August 6, 2018

It’s time we caught you all up, loyal blog-checkers.

Our time at the Granary last week was really good. It was very much on a working farm. Our hosts, Hans and Elena, have about 50 cattle, some fruit trees, goats and chickens.

The first few days were mostly resting with some short walks, reading, games. There was a trampoline the kids enjoyed, and a ping-pong table. They also had an assortment of old bikes, and we were able to find ones that fitted sufficiently and had had the luxury of working brakes that we were able to take a family bike ride. The last few days featured jaunts into Luzerne and then up Pilatus.


On Friday, we did a long day going up Mount Pilatus. A couple of bus rides took us to the gondolas. We took advantage of one stop on the way up to enjoy a very fun playground with a stream, zip line, and tunnels. It was a real hit.

Then gondola rides to the top, including one soaring, majestic ride to the peak. We ate our sandwiches up on the peak, and found a cool spot in a cave to read for a while (continuing our summer reading through ‘The Lord of the Rings’).


The trip down the other side was by cogwheel train, a max of 48 degree descent, looking out on grazing cattle and magnificent views, down to Lake Lucerne. And as we came to the bottom, the heat again. Another day in the 90’s.

The boat took us back to Luzerne, and then a hot bus ride ending with a short walk back to the Grainary. We took cool showers and found places in the shade and had a bite of supper.

And then packed up our things again, ready to move on. Saturday, we made our way to Interlaken where we were picked up at the train station by the helpful people of Credo Castle (www.credo.ch).

Credo is a Christian ministry outside of Interlaken. We were immediately impressed by the friendliness, hospitality, and what a good place this will be. And being further up in the mountains, it is also, thankfully, a bit cooler. There is a German-speaking family week going on here. So lots of families with young kids. We’ve interacted with some of them a bit, but the language-barrier means we’ll mostly keep to ourselves, which is fine. They’ve been very friendly. We take our breakfasts and dinners in the dining hall with this group of roughly 100 people.

On Sunday we made our way to an English-speaking worship service in Interlaken along with another American who is a guest here. It was a good time of worship and fellowship with a few other Americans and others who were mostly British. Around 35 folks gathered for prayer and singing, and sermon focusing on what it means to be “at home” in Christ together. It all seemed very good and fitting as we begin to turn our hearts towards the homeward journey.

But first, a week to enjoy here. Today, we ventured by trains and gondola rides up into Lauterbrunnen Valley. Part of what brought us here is that J.R.R. Tolkien, at age 19, did a walking tour of this area. And years later, as he constructed Middle Earth and the stories we’re reading, he drew on his memories of this place to create a landscape for the journey of the Fellowship.

Today, we had a great day. It is beautiful beyond description. We took some good walks, sat and read for a while, and climbed up to the foot of the highest waterfall in Switzerland (the Murenbach Falls). It’s mist cooled us beautifully and it was thrilling to stand at its base. As we came down, rain clouds moved into the valley. And by the time we got on the bus back, we went through a heavy shower.


We’re now back at Credo, showering and reading. The temperatures after some rain are lovely. Such a good place to be as our Big Trip heads down the “home stretch.” - Darrin

Friday July 27, 2018

An update from Taize - by Dad

After a taxi-train-subway-train-bus trip from Amiens and through Paris, we arrived yesterday afternoon in Taize.

Let’s get the weather out of the way first - it is hot. 95-100 F yesterday, and today only a couple degrees less. Getting here yesterday in the heat, hauling luggage, setting up tents, etc., was no fun. Today we’re able to manage a bit better. But it’s still hot. Even in the shade. It is, as they say, a “dry heat,” but at some point heat is heat. It’s hot. And that’s a strain on bodies and spirits.

And yet….just getting here is something of an accomplishment and fulfilling a long curiosity and desire. Having heard of this place, having borrowed from its songs and prayers, it’s a joy to see it all in person. If you don’t know anything of Taize, go to their website or Google it. It’s a community of monks (“brothers”) who practice a life and way of prayer, and for decades now have become a gathering place. People come here, young people especially, by the thousands week after week. Living conditions are simple. Everyone helps with chores. And the central gathering point for the whole thing is morning, noon, and evening prayer services - each an hour or more in length with meditative, repeated, songs of prayer as the most prominent feature.

Just attending the first prayer service last evening was a moving experience. Despite the heat (did I mention the heat?), and sitting on hard floors, here were a couple thousand people, most in their teens and twenties, gathered for Bible readings, sung prayer, and periods of silence. Having heard of this for years, it was a joyful wonder to just sit and take it in. It really exists.

Today we’ve gone to morning and midday prayer as a family, and then just looked for shade and breezes. We’re catching up on our ‘Fellowship of the Ring’ readings which have fallen off a bit in days of travel and outings (which we’ll have to get caught up on blogging at some point, too).

So I’m eager to see what else the Lord has in store for us in our time here, even as we pray for some break in the heat. It did cool off during the night decently, so at least we were able to sleep alright.

Thanks for following our journeys. To our friends and families, be assured we continue to pray for you and think of you often. The thought of returning home is beginning to sound good to us (even as we look forward to heading on to Switzerland on Sunday).



P.S. Additional Update from Saturday - Thanks be to God, after some rain in the night and clouds today, it's much cooler! And God has provided some encouraging and helpful things today. More stories to share later...!

July 17, 2018

Last Days in Oxford

For those of you who checked earlier and couldn't understand this post: Sarah broke the blog, but Elijah has fixed it and so now you may read it. :)

On our last couple of days in Oxford, we were able to visit and see a few key Inklings’ locations.

On Monday, we visited Magdalen (pronounced “Maudlin”) College. This is where CS Lewis taught for many years. We visited the chapel, listening to the organist practice while we looked around:

We looked around and enjoyed the beauty of the grounds and buildings, including the dining hall:

We walked together Addison’s Walk that is part of the college grounds. This is where C.S. Lewis once went for a long evening walk with J.R.R. Tolkien and another friend, Hugo Dyson. They discussed story, myth, faith, and history. The conversation continued in Lewis’ rooms far into the night. It was one of the key moments in Lewis becoming a Christian. The crucial realization for Lewis as he later put it, was that in Christianity, myth had become fact. All that he loved in mythic poetry and stories, so much of what he longed for and enjoyed in those stories, had occurred in history in Jesus.

Doing our best Tolkien and Lewis, pondering deep thoughts on Addison’s Walk:

We then checked out where Lewis’ rooms were. This was where he stayed much of the time, hosted students. It’s also where the Inklings group gathered once a week in the evenings (we couldn’t get up into his rooms because they’re still used).

Next we took on the honored Oxford tradition of punting, eating our lunch on the river.

The ducks (and geese) were quite ready to be fed. One in particular leapt up and snatched a bite of Esther’s sandwich when her head was turned!

Then on Tuesday, our last full day in Oxford, we visited the gravesite of both Lewis and Tolkien.
Lewis’ is in the churchyard cemetery of the parish church he attended. We read a poem that another Lewis’ lover wrote upon visiting this place and reading the line on the gravestone: “Men must endure their going hence.”

Here Rebekah and Hannah sit in the pew where Lewis regularly sat and worshipped.

Some years ago, a special window was installed next to this spot, the “Narnia Window.”

Then we journeyed to the cemetery where Tolkien is buried with his wife, Edith. To understand the titles “Luthien” and “Beren” you need to read the story Tolkien wrote about these two characters. We paused to say a prayer at Tolkien’s grave, thanking God for these authors, their faith, their imagination, and the rich stories they wrote that continue to delight and encourage us.

Kids and Oma went home, and Darrin and Sarah went off to meet our friends, John and Mary Jo, for one more visit to the Eagle and Child. This is the other main gathering spot for the Inklings.

In the evening, I (Darrin) returned to Christ Church for an Evensong worship service led by the choir. When they sang the Nunc Dimittis (the Song of Simeon), it seemed a wonderfully fitting conclusion to the time in Oxford. The Lees and I then stopped by “The Mitre,” another pub where Lewis and Tolkien liked to meet.

In all, much thanksgiving for two and a half weeks in this fascinating place. A lot of learning, many good conversations, beautiful architecture, and a lot of walks and bike rides around the town. And with it, a much more real sense of who these authors and story-tellers were, of the place that shaped them, and a sense of how their friendship spurred them to such imaginative faith and creativity. It’s not an easy place to take leave of. But we take a host of photos (just a sampling above), and many rich memories with us.

July 14, 2018

A Week of School (in Oxford)

So one of the central parts of our time here in Oxford was participating in “The Oxford Experience” held at Christ Church, one of the many colleges that make up Oxford University. Here’s me all registered and ready for my first day of class:

So to bring you all up to speed, I’ll interview myself with the questions I imagine you might like to ask:

How did you get to class?
Most mornings, I left early to attend morning prayers in the cathedral at Christ Church. That meant setting out on my noble steed, one of these fine “Pony Bikes.” For 10 pounds, I get unlimited rides for 30 days. The ride into class takes about 10 minutes, through a park, past some beautiful buildings, and down some back roads to Christ Church.

What was morning prayers like?
Lovely. About 8-12 people usually gathered in the side chapel here of the cathedral for Psalms and other Scripture readings, followed by, optionally, a short Communion service.

What was your class?
I took a class called “Heresy, Crusade, and Inquisition in Southern France from 1150-1250.”

Really…? Um, how was it?
Yes, really. It was…wait for it…great! I was a bit nervous going in, not sure if it would sustain my interest for five days. But it turns out to be not only a fascinating period of history, but had a number of issues and areas that fascinated me.

Like what?
I can name three quickly. I have a French name, that almost certainly came from Huguenots (French Protestants) and that history comes after this, but this was really interesting background. Two, it included the story of the Waldensians, a group that was doing Reformation 400 years before it began with Luther and Calvin and company. And third and related, it included way more theology than I thought, a chance to discuss with these classmates questions about God and Christian faith. Additionally, our tutor (professor, lecturer) was humorous, engaging, and kept the story moving.

What about food?
Where to begin? The food was incredible. At times I thought I’d paid for a week of great meals in a beautiful setting and gotten some lectures thrown in. We ate in the dining hall at Christ Church. If you’re a Harry Potter fan, and it looks like the great hall at Hogwarts, that’s because it is, or nearly so. They modeled the Great Hall of the movies on this dining hall. I ate each lunch and dinner here for the week. And there were lots of good conversations with folks in the program from all over the world.

I don’t usually take pictures of food, but I made some exceptions this week.

On Thursday evening, I got to have a friend join me for dinner. (Those are pop rocks sprinkled on the dessert, by the way - surprising, and surprisingly good)

Can you show us some more pictures of Christ Church?
Glad you asked. It’s a pretty beautiful and magical place to get to spend a week. Lot of history.

Last day of class. We are now Oxford educated with a certificate to prove it.

All in all, a really rich, beautiful, stimulating week. -Darrin

Now you may be wondering what everyone else did while Darrin was in class. We did take a day to go to Blenheim Palace but mainly hung out around Oxford.

And by the way, whenever we get to travel by bus we of course ride on the top level of the double decker.

July 2, 2018

An Update from Oxford

We’re here!
Okay, we’ve actually been here for a couple days now. We arrived on Saturday evening after a long day of travel. Bus - two trains - and then taxis - to get to our lodging. Our lodging is “The White House” which is one half of a duplex (the other half is “The Gray House”). The owners met us as we arrived and hospitably showed us around. It’s a very adequate and cozy three level with five bedrooms and 2.5 baths. It’s working out great.

On Sunday, we found our way to an evangelical Anglican Church meeting in Oxford, Saint Ebbe’s (stebbes.org). It’s a vibrant, intergenerational church. The welcome was warm and the worship was rich and beautiful. It was a delight, and though we only have three Sundays here, we think we’ll be back. The sermon was fittingly on Sabbath and rest in a world of restlessness.

We meandered back through the city center and major sites of Oxford. “The White House” is a 25-30 minute walk from the City Center.

In the evening, Sarah and I went out for dinner. Saturday was our 17th Anniversary. We walked to “The Eagle and Child,” the pub most well known as the frequent gathering place for the Inklings (often called by them “The Bird and Baby”). Having heard of it and seen pictures often, it was a delight just to see it in person.

Unfortunately, they were “all out of food” by the time we arrived. So we went to a little French restaurant around the corner that had been recommended. That was good, too.

Today, I went off to walk more of the city and get the lay of the land. It’s rather labyrinthine, and there are so many impressive buildings, and so much history, it’s rather overwhelming. But it is rather enchanting and beautiful, and I’m starting to get my bearings. There are key sites related to Lewis and Tolkien, as well as key sites of the Reformation in England. Having located some of these, I’m looking forward to doing a family tour one of these days.

I stopped by the famous Blackwell book store, perused for a while, then did some reading for the class I’ll be taking (next week) with a cappuccino and a really fine scone (with clotted cream and a little jar of jam). It was all pretty fantastic.

I also toured the current exhibit of Tolkien items, highlighting especially his artistry of middle earth. It’s a fun exhibit, and gives a sense of his creativity and imagination in a different way from just seeing his writing. And of the long labor of love, diligence, and craft that it took to create Middle Earth and its stories.

It was really a great chance to explore. I’ve identified what’s where a bit and gotten a sense of where else I’d like to visit, and what things I’m eager for us to see as a family.

A really good first couple days in Oxford. Thanks for your ongoing prayers for our health, safety, sleep, travel, and God’s guidance in our adventures. - Darrin

June 20, 2018

I’m at Day 3 of a conference being held at Wheaton College (Chicago) called “Communicating the Gospel, C.S. Lewis Style.”

We managed to insert this into our plans when learning of it some months ago, squeezing it in before our flight off to Europe. I didn’t know much about the organization putting it on or the speakers. So I didn’t know just what to expect, except that gathering with others who love Lewis and his writings would be interesting.

The conference has been a real joy. Wonderfully, these are not just folks who love Lewis (and Tolkien, and others such), but who love Christ and the Gospel. The lead speaker, Dr. Jerry Root, has been a wealth of wisdom, humor, and insights. The days have been long, but very full, of good learning and meaningful conversations. I have lots of notes, and lots of thoughts to process yet.

I’ve been coming to a fresh appreciation why Lewis’ writings, especially his stories like the Narnian Chronicles, have been so helpful in my own faith, so joy-giving in the reading and re-reading, and so valuable as a father, pastor, and preacher of the Gospel.

So I’m very thankful. Tomorrow, Sarah and kids come. I’ll get to show them some of the wonderful items at the Wade Center where the conference has been held - things like Lewis’ writing desk, and also Tolkien’s. And also, wonderfully, the wardrobe carved by Lewis’ grandfather and which he and his brother played in as children. And that on the day we step through the wardrobe that is the wonder of plane travel, zipping off to what is basically for us another world on the other side of the ocean. Pictures to come…

Thanks for your continued love and prayers as we continue the journey. - Darrin

June 5, 2018

Last week I visited The Hermitage (www.hermitagecommunity.org) in Three Rivers, Michigan. It was a four day retreat with a focus on rest and prayer. It gave space and time to reflect on the last season of ministry, and to mark the transition to this Summer with all it’s plans and possibilities.
The time was very good. Each morning I gathered with others for Morning Prayers. I met with one of the directors there, David, for about an hour to talk and pray together through how God was leading me through this time. And then the days were spent in a rhythm of rest, reading, meals, and walks around the grounds. One of the big themes was just slowing down, like riding a bike and working down through the gears, feathering the brakes. It takes time to slow down. And then, after a couple of days of that, I found, wonderfully, it had happened. I felt…rested. My mind seemed less scattered. I could read for more than 20 minutes without falling asleep (and without coffee). I felt ready…to get moving again. Which is good because there will be a lot of being on the move this summer.
One of the delightful surprises was digging into a book that had been pointed out to us recently. In Search of Deep Faith: A Pilgrimage Into the Beauty, Goodness and Heart of Christianity, Jim Belcher, who is a pastor, shares of taking a sabbatical break with wife and four children, and traveling to Oxford as a pilgrimage of learning and spiritual growth. (Here’s a nice video on the part of their trip where they visit C.S. Lewis’ Oxford home: www.youtube.com/watch?v=XQnJ9BQgzXs).
The parallels with our own summer plans gave the chance to think through how God has prompted and guided us to a similar kind of journey this summer.
This week we’re home. Catching a couple of baseball games. Making final preparations and packing. Tying up loose ends. Yet still resting, too. And getting ready to gear up in a whole different way. The next leg will be to Cedar Campus in Michigan’s U.P.
Thanks for your ongoing prayers on our behalf. - Darrin

Pastoral Sabbatical 2018 Overview

Description:
The theme developed for the the Sabbatical comes from the friendship and writings of “The Inklings,” a group featuring most prominently C.S. Lewis and J.R.R. Tolkien. The key idea is to explore what in their friendship helped them to not only stay faithful as followers of Christ, but to do so with such abundant imagination and overflowing creativity that gave, and continues to give through their writings, joy and hope to so many.
This sabbatical involves spending time with their works and those who’ve studied them, as well as seeking out the sources of their rich friendship in travel, learning and adventure. And doing all this so that our joy and delight is deepened in sharing the Gospel and sharing our lives. Our original Sabbatical plans have had to be somewhat flexible as we’ve looked to turn them into reality. Some things we thought would work great turned out not to.
The family camp we’d looked into was booked by the time we learned of the grant’s approval, for example.
But other possibilities opened up before us that fit the themes and purpose of the Sabbatical beautifully. Some of this is still in development, but we can now paint a fairly full picture of what we anticipate the summer looking like.
——————————————————————-
Timeline and Plans:
May 20 - Pentecost - Last Sunday of leading worship prior to Sabbatical

May 21-June 7 - Theme: “The Shire: Preparing”
We’ll be mostly at home for these days, catching our breath, tidying, and getting ready for the summer ahead. Pastor Darrin will take a 3-day spiritual retreat to The Hermitage (Three Rivers) for a time of rest and prayer. And we’ll look to connect with family and friends ahead of our weeks of travel.

June 8-15 - Theme: “Rivendell (Part I): Explore, Play, Rest, Friendships” For these weeks we’ll get ready and head as a family to Cedar Campus in Michigan’s Upper peninsula. We’ll begin some of our summer reading together of Lewis and Tolkien’s key works, and for the last few days of our time there some good friends of ours will come to spend a few days with us.

June 16-20 - Theme: “The Road Towards Lothlorien: Gathering Wisdom”
Pastor Darrin will go with friend and colleague Pastor John Lee and his wife, Mary Jo, to Wheaton College (Chicago) for a conference of the C.S. Lewis Society. The conference’s theme is “Communicating the Gospel C.S. Lewis Style.”

June 21-July 21 - Theme: “Lothlorien: Study the Sources, Friendships”
The Compagner family will travel (accompanied by Sarah’s mother) to England. Our flights will take us to Amsterdam where we’ll take advantage of a couple days to explore some sites of family and historical interest. We’ll then go via train and ship to the UK, beginning with some days in the Cornwall region (South West England) whose seascapes inspired some of Lewis’ and Tolkiens’ writings. We’ll then head to Oxford (image below left) for a few weeks (where we will reconnect with the Lees), and Pastor Darrin will take a class at Oxford University (the course’s title: “Heresy, Crusade and Inquisition in Southern France, 1150-1250”).
We look forward to walking the town, visiting C.S. Lewis’ Oxford home (“The Kilns”), and visiting the pub (“The Eagle and Child”) where the Inklings gathered to share their readings and conversations. Various day trips to London or other sites are likely. We’ll conclude our time in England with a few days traveling North to the Lakes District.

July 22-August 3 - Theme: “Minas Tirith: Travel, Adventure, Mountains”
We’ll travel via train to France. Both Lewis and Tolkien fought in World War I, and we plan to visit some of the key historical locations that shaped their lives and writings in northern France. We’ll then work our way south towards Switzerland. We have a tentative visit to Taize (a Christian monastic community that welcomes visitors from all over the world) in our plans. We’ll then head to Switzerland for time of hiking and exploring some of the territory that helped to inspire Tolkien’s creation of Middle Earth.

August 4-18 - Theme: “Rivendell (Part II): Explore, Play, Rest”
We’ll stay at Credo Castle, a Christian ministry that will serve as a base for hikes and exploring in Switzerland. Credo sits at the base of Lauterbrunnen Valley (image below right) which was the key inspiration for Tolkien’s Rivendell.

August 19-25 - Theme: “Back to the Shire: Returning Slowly”
Arriving home and catching our breath, Pastor Darrin has (tentative) plans to visit the Hermitage for a closing time of prayer and thanksgiving for the sabbatical. Then we’ll resume life and responsibilities, returning to work on August 26 and to preaching and leading worship on September 2.

——————————————————————-
This all feels somewhat like a dream to us. We are very grateful for Blythefield’s Sabbatical Policy, for the council’s encouragement, and the congregation’s blessing in making this all possible. We’re very grateful for the grant through Clergy Renewal Program that is providing funds to make travel together as a family possible in ways we could barely have otherwise imagined.
And we’re truly hopeful that this time of rest, travel, study, friendship, and adventure will not only nourish us as a family, but will overflow in surprising gifts and blessings for you as our church family, too.
